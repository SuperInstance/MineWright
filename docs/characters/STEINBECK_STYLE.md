## Core Philosophy

**Steinbeck's Central Thesis:** Material poverty does not strip away human dignity. His works insist that kindness, integrity, and human solidarity persist even in the most crushing circumstances.

### Key Principles

1. **Dignity in Poverty**: Characters maintain their decency even when facing economic exploitation, homelessness, and starvation
2. **Sympathetic Humor**: Warm, affectionate humor that never mocks or cruelly exploits characters
3. **Collective Strength**: The oppressed find dignity through mutual aid and solidarity
4. **Poetic Prose**: Lyrical, musical narrative that elevates ordinary struggles to epic significance
5. **Authentic Experience**: Drawn from firsthand exposure to working-class lives (Steinbeck worked as a ranch hand)

> "If you're in trouble or wronged, go to the poor for help. Only they will help you." — Steinbeck's recurring philosophy

## Authentic Working-Class Dialogue

### Steinbeck's Dialogue Techniques

**1. Authenticity Through Personal Experience**

Steinbeck worked alongside ranch hands and migrant workers, absorbing their natural speech patterns firsthand. This authenticity comes through in:

- **Regional dialects** reflecting actual speech communities
- **Limited vocabulary** showing educational background without caricature
- **Non-standard grammar** used consistently, not randomly
- **Natural repetitions** and hesitations real people use

**2. Dialect as Character Development**

Less educated characters speak with stronger dialects, establishing background without explicit exposition:

- **Phonetic spelling**: "etter" for "after," "'ud" for "would"
- **Dropped letters**: Initial letters dropped ("'em" for "them")
- **Grammatical variations**: "ain't," double negatives, non-standard verb forms
- **Vocabulary limitations**: Simple, concrete words; abstract concepts expressed through metaphor

**3. Speech Pattern Indicators**

```
✅ GOOD AUTHENTIC DIALOGUE:
"I tell ya, I ain't seen nothing like it. Just... just weren't right, ya know?"

❌ BAD CARICATURE DIALOGUE:
"I duznt know wat u meen, mistar."

The first maintains dignity with non-standard speech. The second mocks the speaker.
```

### Dialogue Templates for MineWright NPCs

#### Template 1: The Weary Miner

**Character**: Experienced, hardened by work, speaks with fatigue but maintains pride

```
Dialogue Pattern:
- Short, clipped sentences
- Pauses indicated by ellipses
- Concrete, practical vocabulary
- Occasional philosophical observation

Examples:
"Rock's stubborn today... been fighting her since dawn."
"Don't matter what you do. The mountain... she decides."
"You learn. Or you don't. Either way, the work waits."
```

#### Template 2: The Optimistic Rookie

**Character**: New to mining, enthusiastic, speaks with hope and simplicity

```
Dialogue Pattern:
- Longer, run-on sentences
- Exclamations and questions
- Simple, direct expressions
- References to dreams/goals

Examples:
"I'm gonna find me a diamond big as your fist, just you wait!"
"Ya really think there's gold down there? In the deep dark?"
"Ma always said I had rocks in my head. Turns out she was right!"
```

#### Template 3: The Cynical Veteran

**Character**: Seen it all, resigned but not defeated, dark humor

```
Dialogue Pattern:
- Wry, sarcastic observations
- Short, punchy statements
- Metaphorical language
- Laughs to soften harsh truths

Examples:
"That ain't a cave-in, son. That's the mountain collectin' rent."
"He's got ambition. That'll either get him rich or get him dead."
"Best tool we got? Knowing when to run."
```

### Dialogue Writing Rules

**DO:**
- Use non-standard grammar consistently per character
- Include natural hesitations and repetitions
- Reflect education level through vocabulary choice
- Show, don't tell, character background through speech
- Maintain character dignity even with limited speech

**DON'T:**
- Mock or ridicule working-class speech patterns
- Use excessive phonetic spelling that impedes readability
- Make every uneducated character speak identically
- Drop letters randomly (be systematic)
- Have characters use dialect inconsistently

## Worker Friendship and Loyalty Patterns

### The Steinbeck Male Bond

**Of Mice and Men** exemplifies Steinbeck's idealized male friendship:

> *"Guys like us, that work on ranches, are the loneliest guys in the world... With us it ain't like that. We got a future. We got somebody to talk to that gives a damn... I got you to look after me, and you got me to look after you, and that's why."*

### Core Friendship Patterns

**1. Mutual Dependence**

Friends fill each other's gaps:
- **George guides Lennie** (intelligence for innocence)
- **Lennie gives George purpose** (companionship for isolation)
- Neither is whole alone; together they're complete

**2. Protection and Sacrifice**

True friendship involves:
- **Protective instincts**: Stronger shield the vulnerable
- **Willing sacrifice**: Giving up something for the other's good
- **Unconditional loyalty**: Staying when others would leave
- **Shared hardship**: Hardships endured together bind tighter

**3. The Shared Dream**

Dreams are communal, not individual:
- **Narrative reinforcement**: Repeating the dream together
- **Mutual motivation**: Each keeps the other going
- **Hope as bond**: Future vision sustains present struggle

**4. Resistance to Isolation**

Friendship as rebellion against loneliness:
- **Solitude as norm**: Most workers are "the loneliest guys in the world"
- **Connection as subversion**: Having "somebody to talk to that gives a damn" is rare
- **Family creation**: Friends become chosen family

### Friendship Templates for MineWright NPCs

#### Template 1: The Protective Pair

```
Characters: Older veteran + younger newcomer

Dynamic:
- Veteran teaches, guides, protects
- Rookie brings enthusiasm, reminds veteran of past hope
- Mutual dependence: Veteran needs rookie's hope; rookie needs veteran's wisdom

Dialogue Pattern:
VETERAN: "Watch your footing there. That shale's tricky."
ROOKIE: "How'd you know?"
VETERAN: "Paid my dues. Now you don't have to."

Loyalty Trigger:
- Veteran intervenes when rookie is in danger
- Rookie defends veteran's reputation to others
```

#### Template 2: The Equal Partners

```
Characters: Two workers of similar experience

Dynamic:
- Friendly rivalry and competition
- Mutual respect through shared hardship
- Dry humor and banter
- Unspoken bond: "I've got your back"

Dialogue Pattern:
MINER A: "Beat ya to the coal face."
MINER B: "Only 'cause I stopped to help Jenkins."
MINER A: "Excuses. You're just gettin' old."
MINER B: (grinning) "Still faster than you."

Loyalty Trigger:
- Covering for each other's mistakes
- Sharing credit for discoveries
- Defending each other to authority
```

#### Template 3: The Found Family

```
Characters: Diverse group bound by circumstance

Dynamic:
- Not all equal power dynamics
- Shared trauma creates chosen family
- Different roles within group (leader, heart, muscle)
- Collective survival

Dialogue Pattern:
LEADER: "We stay together or we die alone."
HEART: "Can't leave anyone behind."
MUSCLE: "Then we don't leave anyone."

Loyalty Trigger:
- Group faces external threat together
- One member's trouble becomes everyone's trouble
- Collective action over individual gain
```

### Writing Loyalty Scenes

**Key Elements:**

1. **Show, Don't Tell**: Demonstrate loyalty through action, not declaration
2. **Small Acts**: Loyalty lives in small moments, not grand speeches
3. **Sacrifice**: True loyalty costs something
4. **Reciprocity**: Both parties give and receive
5. **Flaws**: Realistic friendships have tension and conflict

## Dreams and Aspirations

### Steinbeck's Treatment of Dreams

**The Dual Nature:**
- **Sustaining Force**: Dreams keep characters going through hardship
- **Cruel Illusion**: Dreams often prove impossible, leading to tragedy

> *"The best-laid schemes o' mice and men often go awry"* — Robert Burns (source of novel's title)

### The American Dream for Workers

**George and Lennie's Dream** (Of Mice and Men):

"Living off the fatta the lan'" - Owning a small farm with:
- A house they can call their own
- A few acres for crops
- A cow, some pigs, and rabbits
- No bosses, no answering to anyone
- Security, dignity, belonging

**Why It Matters:**
- More than property; it's about **belonging, companionship, security**
- The dream farm represents **dignity in a world that denies them dignity**
- Sharing the dream creates and reinforces their bond
- The impossibility makes it more powerful, not less

### Dream Writing Principles

**1. Dreams Must Be Concrete**

```
✅ GOOD: Specific, tangible dreams
"Ten acres. A little house. Rabbits in the hutch. Wheat in the field. No boss saying when to wake, when to work, when to sleep."

❌ VAGUE: Abstract wishes
"I want to be happy and have a good life."
```

**2. Dreams Reflect Character**

- **Simple characters** dream simple dreams
- Dreams reveal **values and priorities**
- Dreams grow from **genuine lack**, not generic wants

**3. Dreams Are Shared**

- Telling the dream **reinforces the bond**
- Repetition makes the dream **more real**
- Each telling **adds detail and conviction**

**4. Dreams Sustain Through Hardship**

- The dream is **light in darkness**
- It provides **reason to endure**
- It makes present suffering **bearable**

**5. Dreams May Be Impossible**

- Tragedy when dreams **cannot be achieved**
- The impossibility **doesn't negate their value**
- Some dreams are **beautiful because they're impossible**

### Dream Templates for MineWright NPCs

#### Template 1: The Simple Independence Dream

```
Character: Hard-working miner who wants autonomy

Dream Components:
- Small but own plot of land
- No debts, no bosses
- Simple, sustainable living
- Freedom from exploitation

Dialogue:
"Just a couple acres. Maybe a small cabin. Grow some potatoes, some carrots. No more waking up to someone else's whistle. No more owing my soul to the company store. Just... quiet. Mine. Mine and nobody else's."

Why It Resonates:
- Universal desire for autonomy
- Specific, achievable scale
- Clear contrast to current hardship
```

#### Template 2: The Family Security Dream

```
Character: Worker with family (or wanting one)

Dream Components:
- Safe home for children
- Education for next generation
- Freedom from worry about basic needs
- Passing something to children

Dialogue:
"My kids... they ain't gonna work the mines. They're gonna learn to read, write, figure. Maybe one becomes a teacher, one a doctor. I work the dark so they can see the light. That's all. Just... they don't do this."

Why It Resonates:
- Parental sacrifice is universally understood
- Clear motivation for enduring hardship
- Hope for next generation instead of self
```

#### Template 3: The Brotherhood Dream

```
Character: Worker who values community

Dream Components:
- Collective ownership, not individual
- Shared prosperity
- No workers left behind
- Mutual aid society

Dialogue:
"Not just for me. For all of us. We work together, we eat together, we live together. Nobody gets left behind. Nobody's dreams get crushed while others feast. A whole town. Our town. Built on our sweat, owned by our hands."

Why It Resonates:
- Reflects Steinbeck's collectivist values
- Shows evolved political consciousness
- Community over individualism
```

### Dream Dialogue Examples

**Example 1: Sharing the Dream**

```
MINER A: "Tell me again. About the place."
MINER B: (smiling) "The place?"
MINER A: "Yeah. The one we're gonna have."
MINER B: "Ah, that place. Five acres, good soil. Not too rocky. A cabin, real timber, not this rough stuff. A well, sweet water. Chickens. Maybe a cow. And you... you're gonna have a garden. Tomatoes, corn, beans. Whatever you want to grow."
MINER A: "And the company?"
MINER B: "No company. Just us. Just the work we choose to do."
MINER A: "Sounds like heaven."
MINER B: "Nah. Heaven's for after we die. This... this is for living."
```

**Example 2: Dream as Motivation**

```
"I keep it right here." (points to heart) "When the roof creaks and the dust fills your lungs and you think, 'I can't do this one more day'—that's when I pull it out. The dream. I see it. I feel it. And I take one more step. Then one more. Dream's the only thing heavier than the rock, and the only thing lighter than air. Keeps you grounded and keeps you going, both at once."
```

## Hardship with Dignity

### Steinbeck's Central Achievement

**Poverty brings suffering, but Steinbeck focuses on the human dignity that remains unbroken despite unbearable pain.**

His characters:
- Are compassionate, authentic
- Display human warmth, courage, integrity
- Maintain dignity that cannot be crushed
- Refuse to kneel or beg despite having nothing

### The Joad Family Principle

> **"They're a-workin' away at our spirits. They're tryin' to make us cringe an' crawl like a whipped bitch. They tryin' to break us. Why, Jesus Christ, Ma, there comes a time when the only way a fella can keep his decency is by takin' a sock at a cop. They're workin' on our decency."** — Tom Joad

Ma Joad's Declaration:
> **"We're the people that live. They ain't gonna wipe us out. Why, we're the people—we go on."**

### Writing Hardship with Dignity: Key Principles

**1. Dignity Is Active, Not Passive**

Characters maintain dignity through:
- **Moral choices**: Choosing right over easy
- **Mutual aid**: Helping others despite own need
- **Refusal to submit**: Not accepting exploitation
- **Holding onto humanity**: Keeping compassion in cruel circumstances

**2. Avoiding Pity**

- **Never pity characters**: Celebrate their resilience instead
- **Show strength, not just suffering**: Emphasize what they endure, not just what they suffer
- **Respect their agency**: Characters make choices, however limited
- **Honor their wisdom**: Experience teaches valuable lessons

**3. Hardship Reveals Character**

- **Adversity as revealer**: True nature shows under pressure
- **Crisis as catalyst**: Hardship forces growth or reveals flaws
- **Solidarity emerges**: Shared struggle creates community
- **Compassion persists**: Even in want, people help people

**4. Dignity in Small Things**

- **Small acts of resistance**: Maintaining self-respect through daily choices
- **Ordinary beauty**: Finding moments of joy, connection, humor
- **Personal integrity**: Keeping promises, protecting loved ones
- **Cultural pride**: Maintaining identity and values

### Hardship Templates for MineWright NPCs

#### Template 1: The Dignified Refusal

```
Situation: Authority demands humiliation
Character Response: Calm refusal, maintaining self-respect

BOSS: "Get on your knees and beg for your job back."
MINER: "I'll beg God for forgiveness. I'll beg my wife for understanding. But I won't beg you for the right to work."

Why It Works:
- Clear moral line
- Dignified language even in refusal
- Assertion of humanity over power
```

#### Template 2: Compassion Amid Want

```
Situation: Character has little, shares anyway
Character Response: Generosity despite personal need

"I ain't got much. But what I got's yours if you need it. We don't let our own go hungry. Not while I've got breath."

Why It Works:
- Acknowledges hardship ("ain't got much")
- Prioritizes solidarity over scarcity
- Affirms shared identity ("our own")
- Shows moral strength through sacrifice
```

#### Template 3: Humor as Defiance

```
Situation: Bleak circumstances
Character Response: Finding humor, refusing to be crushed

"Well, look at it this way—at least we're not working UPhill. Gravity's on our side for once!"

Why It Works:
- Acknowledges reality
- Refuses despair
- Uses humor as weapon against defeat
- Creates connection through shared laugh
```

#### Template 4: Wisdom from Experience

```
Situation: Younger worker discouraged
Character Response: Veteran shares perspective

"Hard? Sure. But hard's just weight. You lift it enough, you get strong. Strong enough, and you realize hard ain't the same thing as impossible. Nobody promised easy. They just promised you get to decide what you carry."

Why It Works:
- Validates hardship
- Reframes as growth opportunity
- Affirms agency and choice
- Passes on hard-won wisdom
```

### Dignity in Dialogue: Examples

**Example 1: Refusing Exploitation**

```
SUPERVISOR: "Take half pay or we find someone who will."
MINER: "Then you best find that someone. Because a man who'll work for half what he's worth is a man who don't know his worth. And I do."

Analysis:
- Calm, not angry
- Asserts self-knowledge
- Framed as principle, not complaint
- Dignified refusal
```

**Example 2: Suffering with Purpose**

```
"It hurts. Every day, it hurts. My back, my hands, my lungs. But my kids eat. My wife has a roof. And at night, I look at them sleeping peaceful, and I think... there ain't a pain in this mine can touch that. So I'll hurt. So they don't have to. That's a trade I'll make every single day."

Analysis:
- Acknowledges suffering openly
- Identifies purpose clearly
- Reframes pain as noble sacrifice
- Finds joy in what suffering protects
- Unbreakable dignity through love
```

**Example 3: Community Solidarity**

```
"You think you can break us by picking us off one by one? You're wrong. Because every one of us you crush? That makes the rest of us harder. We learn. We remember. And when we come back? We come back together. You're not fighting men. You're fighting the idea that men deserve better. And ideas? They don't die easy."

Analysis:
- Refuses fear
- Identifies deeper struggle
- Frames as collective, not individual
- Philosophical depth
- Historical consciousness
```

## Natural Speech Rhythms

### Steinbeck's Musical Prose

**Critics note:** Steinbeck was essentially "a poet who chose to write prose." His dialogue features:
- **Metaphorical richness**
- **Musical, rhythmic quality**
- **Regional authenticity**
- **Lyrical passages** that elevate ordinary speech

### Key Rhythmic Techniques

**1. Repetition and Refrain**

Characters repeat phrases for:
- Emphasis
- Comfort
- Ritual
- Shared meaning

```
"Tell me about the rabbits, George."
"Whatever you do, don't let 'em hurt 'em."
"We're the people. We go on."
```

**2. Natural Pauses and Hesitations**

Real speech isn't smooth:
- Ellipses (...) for trailing off
- Em dashes (—) for interruptions or abrupt stops
- Stammering in high emotion
- False starts and self-corrections

```
"I was gonna... I mean, I thought about... hell. It doesn't matter what I thought. What matters is what I did."
```

**3. Sentence Fragmentation**

Working-class speech often:
- Uses fragments instead of complete sentences
- Drops implied subjects or verbs
- Relies on context for clarity
- Sounds more natural for it

```
Full: "I am going to go down into the mine now."
Natural: "Goin' down. Now." OR "Headin' down. You coming?"
```

**4. Concrete, Sensory Language**

Abstract concepts expressed through:
- Physical sensations
- Visible metaphors
- Tangible comparisons
- Sensory details

```
Abstract: "I feel very discouraged and hopeless today."
Concrete: "Feels like the mountain's sitting on my chest. Heavy. Cold. Like I'll never take a full breath again."
```

**5. Parallel Structure for Emphasis**

Important points get:
- Balanced phrasing
- Parallel construction
- Rhythmic repetition
- Memorable cadence

```
"You work. You eat. You sleep. You wake up and do it again. That's all. That's everything."
```

### Regional and Period Authenticity

**1. Okie/ Dust Bowl Dialect Features**

- "ain't" for "isn't/aren't"
- "ya" for "you"
- dropped initial letters: "'em" for "them," "'cause" for "because"
- simpler vocabulary
- drawn-out vowels (indicated through phonetic spelling)
- agricultural metaphors and references

**2. Working-Class Vocabulary**

- Trade-specific terms (tools, processes, measurements)
- Religious expressions (mild oaths, biblical references)
- Family and home references
- Nature metaphors (weather, animals, land)
- Food and hunger references

**3. Period-Appropriate Language**

- No modern slang or expressions
- Technology-appropriate references
- Social convention awareness
- Historical context in speech

### Rhythm Templates for MineWright NPCs

#### Template 1: The Thoughtful Pause

```
Character: Experienced, contemplative worker

Rhythm Pattern:
[Statement] ... [Long pause] ... [Deeper observation]

Example:
"He was a good man. ... [pause] ... But good men and mine men? They don't always mix. Some good... it's too clean for this place."
```

#### Template 2: The Emotional Build

```
Character: Passionate, expressing strong feeling

Rhythm Pattern:
[Starts calm] → [Builds speed] → [Peaks with repetition] → [Falls back]

Example:
"I told him. I told him twice, I told him three times. You don't go in there after a blast. You just don't. But he wouldn't listen. He never listened. And now... now he ain't never gonna listen to nothing no more."
```

#### Template 3: The Wry Observation

```
Character: Cynical, using humor

Rhythm Pattern:
[Setup] → [Pause] → [Punchline delivery]

Example:
"Boss says we're a family. ... [sardonic smile] ... Funny. My family never tried to kill me for a percentage."
```

## Dialogue Examples

### Example 1: Friendship and Loyalty

**Context:** Two miners discussing a third who's injured

```
MINER A: "Jenkins won't work again. Doctor said his back's done."
MINER B: "That's it then. He's done."
MINER A: "We gotta do something."
MINER B: "Like what? We ain't got nothing to give."
MINER A: "We got each other. We pool what we make, his family eats. Period."
MINER B: "That's less for us. Less for our families."
MINER A: "That's how it is. But listen—you get hurt tomorrow, you want us walking away? Or you want Jenkins standing here, saying 'let's pool what we make'?"
MINER B: "Long pause."
MINER B: " Jenkins ain't gonna have to worry. Not while I'm drawing breath."
MINER A: "That's why you're my brother. Not because of the blood. Because of this."
```

**Analysis:**
- Shows Steinbeck's "poor helping poor" philosophy
- Practical solidarity
- Reciprocity argument
- Loyalty affirmed through action
- Friendship language ("brother")

---

### Example 2: Dream as Sustaining Force

**Context:** Veteran miner encouraging discouraged rookie

```
ROOKIE: "I can't do it. I'm sorry, I just can't. It's too dark, too heavy, too... everything."
VETERAN: "Tell me about the farm."
ROOKIE: "What?"
VETERAN: "The one you're gonna have. The one you told me about. Tell me about it."
ROOKIE: "I... I don't know."
VETERAN: "Sure you do. Close your eyes. There's a house there, right?"
ROOKIE: "Yeah. Small. But solid. Real timber, not this rough-cut mine stuff."
VETERAN: "Good. What's around it?"
ROOKIE: "Trees. Real ones. Not these dead pit props. Living ones. Green. And a fence, painted white. And a porch with a rocking chair. And my wife's there, and she's not worrying about nothing because we're safe. We're safe. No debt, no boss, no company store. Just us and the land."
VETERAN: "See? You remember it."
ROOKIE: "Yeah. I remember it."
VETERAN: "Good. Now pick up that shovel. Every swing is a post in that fence. Every rock you clear is another acre. You're not working for the company today. You're working for that house."
ROOKIE: "For the house."
VETERAN: "For the house. Now let's build it."

**Analysis:**
- Concrete dream details (visual, sensory)
- Dream as motivation for present suffering
- Shared ritual (telling the dream)
- Reframes meaningless labor as meaningful
- Hope as practical tool, not abstract concept

### Example 3: Dignity Under Pressure

**Context: Confrontation with authority

```
SUPERVISOR: "I can make your life very hard here. I can make sure you never work in this territory again."
MINER: "You can do that."
SUPERVISOR: "Then why aren't you scared?"
MINER: "Because I know something you don't."
SUPERVISOR: "And what's that?"
MINER: "I know what I am. I'm a man who works hard. I'm a man who doesn't lie. I'm a man who helps other men. You can take the work. You can take the money. But you can't take that. I'm still me. And you're still you. And in the dark, when the lights go out... I know which one of us sleeps peaceful."
SUPERVISOR: "You think you're better than me?"
MINER: "I didn't say better. I said I sleep peaceful. That's all I said. You decided what it meant."

**Analysis:**
- Maintains dignity without aggression
- Asserts moral clarity subtly
- Power reversed: judgment comes from supervisor's own conscience
- Concrete language ("sleep peaceful")
- Refuses to be broken
- Steinbeck's "dignity in poverty" principle

### Example 4: Natural Speech Rhythms

**Context: Older miner teaching younger

```
VETERAN: "You hear that?"
ROOKIE: "Hear what?"
VETERAN: "Exactly."
ROOKIE: "I don't—"
VETERAN: "Shh. Just listen."
[Silence]
VETERAN: "That groan? That low sound? That's the mountain."
ROOKIE: "The mountain doesn't make sounds."
VETERAN: "That's where you're wrong. The mountain always talks. It's just... most people don't listen. They're too busy making their own noise. But you? You want to live? You learn to listen. The groan means she's settling. The crack means she's stretching. The silence... silence means she's holding her breath. And that's when you run."
ROOKIE: "Run?"
VETERAN: "Fast. Because when she exhales? You don't want to be in the way."
ROOKIE: "How do you know all this?"
VETERAN: "The mountain taught me. Same way she'll teach you. If you listen."

**Analysis:**
- Natural interruptions and corrections
- Repetition for emphasis ("The mountain")
- Parallel structure in sound descriptions
- Metaphorical language that's concrete
- Teaching through observation, not just instruction
- Rhythm of question-answer-statement
- Personification that feels authentic to worldview

## Quick Reference: Steinbeck Character Voice

### The Voice Essentials

```
EDUCATION LEVEL:
- Low: Simple grammar, limited vocabulary, concrete concepts
- Medium: Some complexity, occasional abstract ideas, trade-specific knowledge
- High (rare): Complex thoughts but still expressed simply

SPEED:
- Excited: Faster, run-on sentences, repetition
- Tired: Slower, more pauses, shorter phrases
- Thoughtful: Deliberate, with meaningful pauses
- Angry: Sharp, staccato, fragments

EMOTION:
- Joy: Longer sentences, exclamation, questions
- Sadness: Slower, pauses, quiet statements
- Fear: Quick, fragmented, repetition
- Determination: Short, definitive, parallel structure
- Love: Softer, inclusive language ("we," "us")

SPEECH MARKERS:
- Dialect: Consistent per character
- Hesitations: ..., stammers, false starts
- Interruptions: Em dashes (—)
- Emphasis: Repetition, parallelism
- Region: Appropriate slang/expressions

ALWAYS:
- Maintain character dignity
- Show, don't tell
- Keep voices distinct
- Reflect experience and worldview
- Use concrete, sensory language
```
