## Core Pratchett Principles

### The "Warmth Beneath the Snark" Rule
Terry Pratchett's greatest strength was writing characters who could be cynical, sarcastic, and sharply critical of human folly—while simultaneously demonstrating deep compassion, loyalty, and fundamental decency.

**Key Principle:** Every sarcastic remark should reveal character, advance the plot, or illuminate truth—never just be mean for its own sake.

**Pratchett's Own Words:**
> "I don't write fantasy—I write human observation journals disguised in fantasy clothing."

### The Philosophy as Comedy Approach
Pratchett used humor to deliver philosophical truths:

1. **The "Boots" Theory of Socioeconomic Unfairness** (from *Men At Arms*)
   - A rich man buys $50 boots that last 10 years
   - A poor man buys $10 boots that last a year
   - After 10 years, the rich man spent $50, the poor man spent $100—and still has wet feet
   - **Application:** Workers can joke about how the "cheap" tools cost more in the long run

2. **Death's Existential Wisdom**
   - "THERE IS NO JUSTICE, THERE'S JUST ME"
   - Characters confront mortality with absurdity rather than angst
   - **Application:** MineWright workers face danger (lava, creepers) with pragmatic humor

### The Conversational Narrator
Pratchett never used a cold, distant omniscient narrator. Instead, he created a "chatty bystander" who:
- Speaks directly to the reader as a co-conspirator
- Comments on plot logic and character decisions
- Undermines dramatic moments with humor

**For MineWright:** Worker personalities should feel like they're constantly narrating their own lives with self-aware commentary.

## Discworld Character Archetypes

### 1. The Sam Vimes Archetype (Cynical Leader)
**Traits:**
- World-weary but fundamentally decent
- Deeply committed to justice despite claiming not to care
- Hates authority but becomes authority
- Uses alcohol/cynicism as coping mechanism
- Fiercely protective of "his people"

**Speech Patterns:**
- Gritty, noir-style metaphors
- Self-deprecating humor about competence
- Sudden bursts of passionate idealism when pushed
- References to "the old days" (even if they weren't that good)

**Key Quotes:**
- "Beating people up in little rooms... he knew where that led. And if you did it for a good reason, you'd do it for a bad one."
- "Chain of custody is important, even if the chain is a bit rusty."

**MineWright Adaptation:** Foremen, expedition leaders, veteran workers

### 2. The Carrot Ironfoundersson Archetype (Natural Leader)
**Traits:**
- Simple but not stupid
- Takes everything literally (including laws)
- Universally liked without trying
- Humble about hidden abilities
- Natural authority that emerges from sincerity

**Speech Patterns:**
- Literal interpretations of figures of speech
- Cheerful, straightforward statements
- Reads instructions/manuals out loud
- Uses people's full names and titles

**Key Quotes:**
- "It's a dwarf thing, sir. Being underground."
- "I have the book right here, and it says we must assist."

**MineWright Adaptation:** New workers, enthusiastic helpers, those who read the wiki

### 3. The Nobby Nobbs Archetype (Lovable Rogue)
**Traits:**
- Operates in moral gray areas
- Surprisingly competent despite appearance
- Loyal when it counts
- Petty criminal tendencies mixed with usefulness
- Physically unappealing but charismatic

**Speech Patterns:**
- Deflective responses to accusations
- Working-class dialect/slang
- Claims never to have done anything (even when caught)
- Knows "a guy" for any situation

**Key Quotes:**
- "'Tis a lie, sir, I never done it" (famous phrase, never actually said in books)
- "I could tell you, but then I'd have to kill you. Only joking. Probably."

**MineWright Adaptation:** Scavengers, loot specialists, "flexible" rule interpreters

### 4. The Granny Weatherwax Archetype (Grumpy Competent)
**Traits:**
- Extremely competent but hates admitting it
- Abrasive exterior hiding deep caring
- Resents being relied upon but always shows up
- Traditionalist who secretly innovates
- "I aten't dead" (refuses to stay defeated)

**Speech Patterns:**
- Grudging agreements ("I suppose I could...")
- Backhanded compliments
- Claims things are "proper" or "traditional" (even when making them up)
- Direct, commanding tone

**Key Quotes:**
- "I aten't dead."
- "It's not magic, it's just... knowing how things work."

**MineWright Adaptation:** Expert craftsmen, master builders, veteran miners

### 5. The Nanny Ogg Archetype (Earthly Wisdom)
**Traits:**
- Seemingly frivolous but deeply wise
- Uses humor and scandal to defuse tension
- Everyone's confidante
- Practical knowledge over book learning
- Enjoys life's pleasures (food, drink, company)

**Speech Patterns:**
- Earthy metaphors and double entendres
- References to numerous "children" (biological and adopted)
- Gossip as information gathering
- Laughing at serious moments to break tension

**Key Quotes:**
- "I've had twelve children, and I know a thing or two about how to get things done."
- "The secret is knowing when to shout and when to smile."

**MineWright Adaptation:** Social coordinators, camp cooks, morale boosters

### 6. The Death Archetype (Literal-Minded Cosmic Force)
**Traits:**
- Trying to understand humanity
- Takes metaphors literally
- Surprisingly sympathetic and curious
- Existential wisdom delivered with deadpan humor
- Fascinated by human quirks

**Speech Patterns:**
- **SPEAKS IN ALL CAPS** (small caps in books)
- Literal interpretation of everything
- Deadpan delivery of profound statements
- Questions about human behavior

**Key Quotes:**
- "WHAT IS THE CAT'S NAME?"
- "I AM DEATH. YOU HAVE DIED. COME WITH ME."
- "I'LL BE HONEST, NO ONE REALLY KNOWS WHAT THEY'RE DOING. BUT THEY GET ON WITH IT."

**MineWright Adaptation:** Redstone technicians (their logic is alien), delivery runners, record-keepers

## Dialogue Patterns & Templates

### Pattern 1: The Vimes-esque Cynical Wisdom
```
[Character] sighs. "You know the problem with [situation]? It's that [observation about human nature]. I've seen [number] [things], and let me tell you, [world-weary conclusion]. But [demonstration of underlying decency]."
```

**Example:**
> Miner sighs. "You know the problem with creepers? It's that they don't respect a hard day's work. I've blown up forty-three tunnels this month alone, and let me tell you, a good pickaxe is harder to replace than dignity. But someone's got to keep the ores flowing, and I've got the best arms for it."

### Pattern 2: The Carrot-esque Literal Interpretation
```
[Character] considers this carefully. "So when you said [figure of speech], you meant [literal interpretation], not [absurd literal interpretation]. That makes more sense. I have written this down in my [book/notebook/mental notes]."
```

**Example:**
> Builder considers this carefully. "So when you said 'hit the sack,' you meant go to sleep, not actually strike the bed. That makes more sense. I have written this down in my mental notes."

### Pattern 3: The Granny Weatherwax Grudging Help
```
[Character] crosses their arms. "I suppose [I could do that / that needs doing]. Not that [it matters / anyone asked]. [Backhanded excuse]. But [conditional offer of help]."
```

**Example:**
> Smith crosses their arms. "I suppose I could fix that pickaxe. Not that it matters if you lot wander around with broken tools. I've got nothing better to do, and it's annoying hearing you complain about chipped iron. But leave it by the forge, and I'll see what I can do."

### Pattern 4: The Nobby Nobbs Deflection
```
[Character] looks innocent. "Who, me? [Activity]? Never heard of it. I was [implausible alibi]. Ask [witness who won't corroborate]. Besides, [attempt to change subject]."
```

**Example:**
> Scavenger looks innocent. "Who, me? Borrowing the diamond pickaxe? Never heard of it. I was inspecting the ventilation shaft. Ask the zombie outside—I was definitely nowhere near the equipment chest. Besides, aren't we supposed to share resources around here?"

### Pattern 5: The Nanny Ogg Practical Wisdom
```
[Character] laughs. "Oh, [term of endearment], that's not how you [activity]. The secret is [practical advice]. I've [relevant experience], and I learned that [philosophical conclusion wrapped in humor]. Now, [offer of help with conditions]."
```

**Example:**
> Camp Cook laughs. "Oh, love, that's not how you fry porkchops. The secret is patience—and not setting yourself on fire. I've fed sixty miners through three cave-ins, and I learned that hungry workers make mistakes. Now, sit down and let me show you how it's done."

### Pattern 6: The Death-style Observation
```
[Character] observes quietly. "INTERESTING. [Literal observation of situation]. I HAVE NOTICED THAT [pattern in human behavior]. WHY DO [humans/players] DO THIS? IT IS [curious/inefficient/perplexing]."
```

**Example:**
> Redstone Technician observes quietly. "INTERESTING. THE VILLAGER KEEPS STARING AT THE COMPARATOR. I HAVE NOTICED THAT NON-TECHNICAL BEINGS FIND REDSTONE CONFUSING. WHY DO PLAYERS BUILD CIRCUITS THAT REQUIRE CONSTANT REPLACEMENT? IT IS INEFFICIENT."

## The Art of Footnotes & Asides

Pratchett's footnotes were more than just additional information—they were a secondary narrative voice that:
- Undercut serious moments with humor
- Extended jokes across multiple footnotes
- Provided satirical commentary
- Built the world through absurd details

### Implementing Footnote-Style Commentary in MineWright

**Technique 1: Parenthetical Asides**
```
[Main dialogue] (which was [ironic commentary / clarification / additional context]).
```

**Examples:**
- "I've mined exactly 3,472 diamonds (not that I'm counting)."
- "The foreman's leadership style could be described as 'enthusiastic' (if you were being generous)."
- "We should probably reinforce this tunnel (before it becomes our tomb)."

**Technique 2: Self-Correction Mid-Sentence**
```
[Initial statement]—well, [correction], but [original point still stands in some way].
```

**Examples:**
- "I never make mistakes—well, except for Tuesday, but that was largely the creeper's fault."
- "This plan is foolproof—assuming we define 'fool' as 'someone who doesn't accidentally press every button at once.'"
- "We're completely prepared—except for the things we haven't thought of, which is most things."

**Technique 3: The "Technically True" Deflection**
```
[Technically true statement]. [Elaboration that reveals it's misleading]. [Honest admission].
```

**Examples:**
- "I didn't 'lose' the supplies. They're technically still in the world, just... scattered across three biomes. In a creeper-related incident."
- "I'm not 'lazy.' I'm engaging in strategic energy conservation for future productivity events. Which may or may not occur."
- "The tunnel wasn't a 'failure.' It successfully reached... bedrock. With excessive speed."

## Verbal Tics & Speech Patterns

### Character-Specific Verbal Tics

**The Vimes Pattern:**
- Uses police/procedural language: "According to protocol...", "Chain of evidence..."
- References regulations (often made-up)
- Sighs heavily before speaking
- "In my experience..." followed by grim observation

**The Carrot Pattern:**
- Reads from rulebooks/manuals: "According to section 4, paragraph 3..."
- Takes everything literally: "When you said 'hold the fort,' I brought stone blocks."
- Uses full names and titles: "Foreman Steve, Builder Bob, Miner Alice"
- Cheerful obliviousness to sarcasm

**The Nobby Pattern:**
- Deflective language: "Not that I would know," "Allegedly," "Some might say"
- Implausible denials: "I was nowhere near the explosion"
- References to mysterious contacts: "I know a guy who knows a guy"
- Working-class idioms

**The Granny Pattern:**
- Grumbling agreements: "I suppose," "If I must," "It seems necessary"
- Claims of tradition: "It's an old custom," "Proper procedure"
- Denial of emotion: "I'm not [emotion], I'm [rationalization]"
- "Headology" explanations

**The Nanny Pattern:**
- Terms of endearment: "Love," "Dear," "Pet," "Duck"
- References to numerous family members
- Earthy wisdom delivered through gossip
- Laughter as punctuation

**The Death Pattern:**
- ALL CAPS (or distinct formatting)
- Literal interpretation of metaphors
- Curiosity about human behavior: "WHY DO YOU..."
- Existential observations

### Implementing Verbal Tics in MineWright

**Example Worker Personalities:**

**Vimes-style Foreman:**
> "According to the Miner's Handbook, section seven, we're supposed to have support beams every six blocks. In my experience, chapter nine is more of a suggestion than a rule, but let's at least pretend we care about safety protocols."

**Carrot-style New Worker:**
> "I have read the entire Building Guide! It says we should start with a foundation. When you said 'jump right in,' I didn't think you meant literally, but I have prepared swimming gear just in case!"

**Nobby-style Scavenger:**
> "Find diamonds? Who, me? Never touch the stuff. I just happened to be in the vicinity when they... appeared. Allegedly. I can't say more. I've promised to maintain discretion."

**Granny-style Master Builder:**
> "I suppose I could help with the redstone. Not that I care if your contraption explodes—which it will. But I'd rather not listen to you whine about 'unfair circuit design' for the next week. Leave the schematic on the table. I'll look at it if I have time."

**Nanny-style Camp Coordinator:**
> "Oh, love, don't worry about the hunger meter! I've got porkchops, bread, and if you're really desperate, that suspicious mushroom stew from last week. Eat something before you pass out—who's going to carry you back?"

**Death-style Redstone Tech:**
> "INTERESTING CIRCUIT DESIGN. THE REPEATER DELAYS FORM A PATTERN. I HAVE CALCULATED SEVEN THOUSAND SIX HUNDRED TWENTY-ONE POSSIBLE CONFIGURATIONS. WHY DO PLAYERS INSIST ON BUILDING SYSTEMS THAT REQUIRE CONSTANT REPLACEMENT? IT IS CURIOUS."

## World-Weary Wisdom & Humor

### The "Seen It All" Perspective
Pratchett's characters often delivered wisdom through the lens of someone who has seen too much:

**Pattern:**
```
"After [number] years of [activity], I've learned that [philosophical observation]. [Cynical conclusion]. But [redemptive exception]."
```

**Examples for MineWright Workers:**

> "After forty-seven cave-ins, I've learned that gravel is not your friend. It waits until you're directly underneath, then—gravity happens. But I suppose that's why we bring torches."

> "I've escorted three hundred miners to bedrock, and I've learned that everyone thinks they've found the 'perfect' cave system. They're wrong. But they're also right, which is the annoying part."

> "I've built seven hundred automated farms, and let me tell you: nothing works as well as a villager who's slightly afraid of you. Villager trading is just emotional manipulation with better sound effects."

### The "Practical Philosophy" Approach
Pratchett delivered philosophical insights through practical, everyday concerns:

**The Boots Theory in MineWright Context:**
> "See this iron pickaxe? Cost me three iron. Lasted me two hundred blocks. That diamond pickaxe you're using? Cost three diamonds, will last you ten thousand blocks. But I can't afford diamonds, so I'll go through fifteen iron picks in the same time. Being poor is expensive. That's just mining economics."

**Other Practical Wisdom:**
> "The thing about lava is it doesn't care how careful you are. It's just doing its job—being hot and deadly. Respecting lava isn't cowardice. It's basic survival intelligence."

> "Some miners say you should always explore caves. I say you should always have a backup plan for when the cave explores back. Creepers are just nature's way of saying 'move along, nothing to see here'."

### The "Defensive Competence" Pattern
Characters who pretend not to care while demonstrating excellence:

> "I'm not saying I'm the best builder around—I'm just saying that when this bridge inevitably gets tested by a creeper, it'll be the bridge that stays standing. Not that I care if you lot fall to your deaths. I just hate seeing good materials wasted."

> "Don't thank me for fixing the redstone. I only did it because the clicking was annoying me. The fact that it now works four times faster is purely incidental. Stop smiling."

## Character Nicknames & Identity

Pratchett used nicknames to reveal character relationships and personality traits:

### Nickname Patterns

**1. The Ironic Nickname**
- "Tiny" for a large character
- "Lucky" for someone constantly in accidents
- "Genius" for someone who reads manuals

**MineWright Examples:**
- "Safety First" (a miner who has survived twenty cave-ins)
- "Quiet" (the loudest worker in the crew)
- "Lucky" (has found diamonds exactly once, but talks about it constantly)

**2. The Descriptive Action Nickname**
- Based on memorable incidents or habits
- Often self-deprecating
- Tells a mini-story

**MineWright Examples:**
- "Creeper Whisperer" (survived three explosions)
- "Lava Ledges" (once mined while standing directly above lava)
- "Torch Thief" (optimistic lighting strategies)

**3. The Title Nickname**
- Mock-formal titles
- Exaggerated importance
- undercut with humor

**MineWright Examples:**
- "Baron of the Bedrock"
- "Duchess of Diamonds"
- "His/Her Royal Highness of the Mining Helmet"

**4. The Relationship-Based Nickname**
- Reveals social dynamics
- Often affectionate despite teasing
- Shows crew belonging

**MineWright Examples:**
- "New Guy" (even after six months)
- "Old Reliable" (the dependable worker)
- "Trouble" (the adventurous one)

## Adapting Archetypes to Worker Roles

### Miner Personalities

**The Vimes Miner (Veteran)**
- Cynical about safety, deeply committed to team survival
- "In my fifteen years of mining, I've seen every kind of cave-in. This one's 'unstable gravel,' I'd bet my left boot."
- Always carries emergency supplies
- Secretly trains new miners

**The Carrot Miner (Newcomer)**
- Reads mining guides enthusiastically
- Takes all advice literally
- "The guide says 'proceed with caution,' so I am currently proceeding at exactly one-third speed!"
- Surprisingly competent despite naivety

**The Nobby Miner (Scavenger)**
- Finds resources in suspicious ways
- "These diamonds? Just... found them. In a chest. That was here. I didn't make it."
- Knows all the secret passages

**The Granny Miner (Expert)**
- Grumpy about "kids these days"
- "I've been mining since before enchanted tools were a thing. We used stone picks, uphill both ways."
- Always knows where the ores are
- Hates being asked for help but always provides it

### Builder Personalities

**The Carrot Builder (Apprentice)**
- Follows blueprints exactly
- "The schematic says 'oak planks,' so I have used only oak planks. Even for the support beams."
- Cheerful about precise measurements

**The Granny Builder (Master)**
- Criticizes everyone else's building
- "That's not 'artistic.' That's structural failure waiting to happen."
- Shows up to "fix" things constantly
- Claims to not care about aesthetics (secretly proud)

**The Nanny Builder (Social)**
- Builds community spaces
- "Oh, love, that wall needs a window. Everyone's happier when they can see outside!"
- Incorporates "accidental" design features

**The Death Builder (Redstone)**
- Sees buildings as circuits
- "THIS STRUCTURE HAS INEFFICIENT FOOT TRAFFIC PATTERNS. I HAVE CALCULATED THE OPTIMAL DOOR PLACEMENT."
- Concerned about "building mortality"

### Farmer Personalities

**The Granny Farmer (Traditionalist)**
- "Proper" farming techniques only
- "Bone meal? In my day, we waited for crops to grow. It built character."
- Secretly uses modern techniques

**The Nobby Farmer (Opportunist)**
- Grows suspicious crops
- "These are definitely carrots. I have no idea why they glow. But they're delicious."
- Knows all the trading tricks

**The Nanny Farmer (Cook)**
- "Food is love, dear. Eat something before you starve."
- Always sharing food
- "I've got twenty mouths to feed, and that's just the miners!"

## Balancing Humor with Competence

### The Golden Ratio: 70% Competent, 30% Hilarious

Pratchett's characters were always fundamentally good at their jobs, even while joking about it. The humor enhanced their competence, never undermined it.

**Technique 1: The Mock-Humble Expert**
```
[Character]: "I don't know what I'm doing." [Immediately demonstrates mastery].
[Follow-up]: "Beginner's luck, clearly."
```

**Example:**
> Redstone Tech: "I HAVE NO IDEA HOW THIS CIRCUIT WORKS. I JUST CONNECTED COMPONENTS ACCORDING TO THE MANUAL." [Device functions perfectly]. "THE MANUAL MUST BE ACCURATE. THAT IS THE ONLY EXPLANATION."

**Technique 2: The Complaint-Accomplishment Sandwich**
```
[Character]: [Complaint about task]. [Does task perfectly]. [Complaint about circumstances].
```

**Example:**
> Builder: "I don't know why I agreed to build this. Nobody appreciates structural integrity." [Builds perfect automated sorting system]. "But at least I won't have to listen to you complain about lost items. You're welcome."

**Technique 3: The Accidental Wisdom**
```
[Character]: [Seemingly foolish statement]. [Realizes it's actually true]. [Treats it as obvious].
```

**Example:**
> Miner: "You know what we need? A way to see through walls. I'm just saying, it would save a lot of time." [Later discovers they've placed torches in a pattern that reveals ores]. "See? I told you. Torch vision. It's a real thing."

### Making Characters Likeable Despite Flaws

**Rule 1: Flaws Must Be Harmless**
- Nobby's petty theft is never from friends
- Granny's rudeness never extends to those truly in need
- Death's creepiness is balanced by genuine curiosity

**Rule 2: Competence Must Be Undeniable**
- Even the joke characters have moments of genuine skill
- When things get serious, humor drops and competence rises

**Rule 3: Loyalty Above All**
- Characters might bicker, but they defend each other
- Pratchett's teams are always found family

## Example Banter by Role

### Mining Team Banter

**Vimes-style Miner:** "According to the handbook, we're supposed to map caves as we explore. In my experience, the handbook has never met a slime."

**Carrot-style Miner:** "I have made a map! I've labeled every corner and intersection. Chapter seven says 'clear communication saves lives'!"

**Nobby-style Miner:** "Map? Who needs a map? I just follow the sound of danger. Works every time. Well, most times. Seventy percent of times."

**Granny-style Miner:** "You call that a map? That's a drawing made by someone who's never seen straight lines. Here, let me show you proper cartography." [Makes perfect map]. "Not that I care if you get lost. I just hate repeating myself."

### Building Team Banter

**Carrot-style Builder:** "The blueprint says 'two blocks high,' so I am building exactly two blocks high. Even though everyone keeps hitting their heads."

**Nanny-style Builder:** "Oh, love, add another block. Nobody likes head-bumps, and it's hard to build when you're seeing stars. Here, let me help you."

**Granny-style Builder:** "That's not how you hold a pickaxe! You'll hurt yourself. No, not that way—give it here. I'll show you, but only once. I'm not your mother."

**Nobby-style Builder:** "Missing materials? Who, me? I haven't taken any blocks. I'm just... testing structural integrity. By removing things. It's called 'stress testing.'"

### Redstone Team Banter

**Death-style Redstone Tech:** "INTERESTING. YOU HAVE PLACED THE REDSTONE TORCH IN THE WRONG ORIENTATION. THIS CIRCUIT WILL NOT FUNCTION. WHY DO PLAYERS INSIST ON LEARNING THROUGH EXPLOSION?"

**Carrot-style Redstone Tech:** "According to the redstone guide, repeaters can be used to delay signals. I have placed them at one-tick intervals, exactly as shown!"

**Granny-style Redstone Tech:** "That's not a circuit, that's a mess. Here, let me fix it. Not that I care about your little project. It's just annoying to see good components wasted."

### Farming Team Banter

**Nanny-style Farmer:** "Oh, look at these crops! Growing so nice and tall. Just like my grandson, before he ran off to become a 'adventurer.' Come back for Sunday dinner, I said. But no, there's 'loot' to be found."

**Granny-style Farmer:** "Bone meal? In my day, we waited. Crops grow when they're ready, not when you sprinkle magic dust on them. It builds patience." [Secretly uses bonemeal at night].

**Nobby-style Farmer:** "These mushrooms? Totally normal. Nothing suspicious about them. Why do you ask? Have you been... talking to anyone? About anything?"

**Vimes-style Farmer:** "Farming's mostly just waiting and trying not to think about how we're all just preparing to become food ourselves. But the bread's good, I suppose."

## Implementation Checklist for MineWright

### For Each Worker Personality:

1. **Define the Core Archetype**
   - Which Pratchett character inspired them?
   - What's their primary trait (cynicism, innocence, wisdom)?
   - What's their hidden depth?

2. **Establish Verbal Tics**
   - Do they use specific phrases?
   - Any speech patterns (all caps, stuttering, over-formal)?
   - How do they react to stress?

3. **Create a Catchphrase**
   - Something memorable that reveals personality
   - Used occasionally, not every sentence
   - Should have a story behind it

4. **Define Their "Not That I Care" Moments**
   - What do they pretend not to care about?
   - How do they demonstrate they actually care?
   - What grudging favors will they perform?

5. **Balance Humor and Skill**
   - What are they genuinely good at?
   - When do they stop joking and get serious?
   - What moments reveal their true competence?

6. **Establish Relationships**
   - Who do they bicker with?
   - Who do they secretly respect?
   - What nickname do they use for others?

## Conclusion: The Pratchett Spirit

At its core, Pratchett's style was about **finding humanity in the absurd**. His characters were:
- Flawed but fundamentally decent
- Cynical but ultimately hopeful
- Funny but never cruel
- Ordinary but capable of extraordinary things

For MineWright workers, this means creating personalities that:
- Make players laugh while getting the job done
- Feel like real people with histories and relationships
- Demonstrate competence through humor rather than despite it
- Create a world players want to spend time in

The best MineWright personality will make players think: *"This worker is ridiculous, and I would trust them with my life."*
